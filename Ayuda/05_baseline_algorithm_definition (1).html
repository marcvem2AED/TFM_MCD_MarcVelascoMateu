Baseline Algorithm Definition#

This section describes the baseline SWF retrieval algorithm. The algorithm is based on the methodology proposed by [Du et al., 2016, Du et al., 2017, Du et al., 2018], adapted to the available microwave channels and specific overpass time of CIMR.

Retrieval Method#

The Tau-Omega model equation (10) describes the surface emissivity of terrestrial surfaces \(e_p\) as observed by satellites under conditions where the land is not frozen and free of snow [Jones et al., 2010].

(10)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} & \varepsilon_p = f_w \cdot \varepsilon_{pw} + (1-f_w) \cdot \varepsilon_{pl} \\ & \varepsilon_{pl} = (1-\omega_p)(1-\gamma_p)\left[1 + R^S_p \gamma_p \right] + (1-R^S_p)\gamma_p \\ & \gamma_p = \exp[-\tau_p] \\ & \varepsilon_{pw} = f(\varepsilon_w, S_r) \end{align*}\end{split}\]

where the subscript \(p\) indicates polarization, with \( \varepsilon_{w}\) and \( \varepsilon_{l}\) representing the emissivities of water and land, respectively. The surface water term \(f_w\) refers to the fractional water within the sensor field of view (i.e., Surface Water Fraction, SWF). The symbol \( \gamma\) denotes the microwave transmissivity through the vegetation layer. \(\tau\) refers to the vegetation optical depth at the polarization p (H or V). \( R^{s}\) represents the soil effective microwave reflectivity, incorporating the influence of surface roughness. \( \varepsilon_{w}\) is the permittivity of water, and \( S_{r}\) is the surface water roughness.

Although the Tau-Omega model provides a theoretical framework to describe the relationship between surface emissivity and water fraction, applying it directly for SWF retrieval requires knowledge of multiple surface parameters, including vegetation optical depth (VOD), vegetation single scattering albedo, soil roughness and dielectric properties.

Due to the complexity and limited availability of these input parameters at global scale and high resolution, a simplified retrieval method is proposed. This method is based on the use of a Difference Ratio (DR), which approximates SWF from observed surface emissivity and pre-computed reference emissivities for water and land, as showed in the mathematical description presented below.

Reference Emissivity Estimation for Land and Water Endmembers#

A Look-Up Table (LUT) containing reference K-band microwave emissivities was developed to characterize pure endmember conditions for land and water. The procedure was divided into two main steps:

Step 1: Estimation of reference emissivity for freshwater (water endmember)#

To obtain a reliable value for the open water emissivity \((\varepsilon_{hw}^{\text{ref}})\), a fixed reference was estimated using observations exclusively from pixels fully located within large freshwater lakes. These pixels were carefully selected to avoid contamination from mixed land–water interfaces or coastal effects.

The emissivity was calculated as:

(2)#\[\varepsilon = \frac{TB_h}{T_{\text{skin}}}\]

Where:

  • \(TB_h\) is the observed brightness temperature from WindSat at horizontal polarization (h-pol), in Kelvin.

  • \(T_{\text{skin}}\) is the surface skin temperature obtained from ECMWF.

This calculation assumes clear-sky conditions and negligible atmospheric effects. The resulting mean emissivity for open freshwater bodies was estimated to be:

\[\varepsilon_{hw}^{\text{ref}} = 0.398\]

This value was treated as constant for the water endmember, given the low variability of freshwater emissivity. It should be noted that the LUT will require reprocessing once CIMR in-flight data becomes available.

Step 2: Estimation of reference emissivities for land (land endmember)#

To construct the LUT for land emissivity, global estimates were derived using WindSat brightness temperature (TB) data in combination with the K-band Surface Water Fraction (SWF) product from the LPDR dataset [Du et al., 2017]. The SWF product served as a screening criterion to ensure that only pure land surfaces were used in the analysis.

Pure land endmembers were identified as grid cells where the SWF value was below 0.02, indicating negligible surface water influence. For these pixels, the surface emissivity at horizontal polarization was computed using the same equation described previously ((2)).

Unlike the water case where a fixed emissivity value was sufficient, land emissivity exhibits considerable variability depending on surface conditions. To account for this, a Look-Up Table (LUT) of reference land emissivities was constructed across discrete intervals of Soil Moisture (SM) and Vegetation Optical Depth (VOD). All pure land pixels were binned according to their SM and VOD values, and the corresponding emissivity values within each bin were averaged. These mean values were then used to fill the LUT with representative reference emissivities for each SM–VOD combination (Table 1).

CIMR Level-1b pre-processing and re-sampling approach#

The CIMR SWF processor ingests L2PP Land files produced by the CIMR L1B/L2Bridge. These files contain non-water corrected K-band TB measurements (V and H polarization) observations in swath geometry, corrected for OZA variations across feeds and along the scan.

The bridge also provides atmospheric parameters to convert top-of-atmosphere TBs to bottom-of-atmosphere.

L2PP TBs are resampled to a fixed Earth grid using the RGB toolbox in a 3 km EASE global grid.

Note

Atmospheric correction should be integrated into the pre-processing. Additional implementation details will be included in a future ATBD release.

Algorithm Assumptions and Simplifications#

By calculating land from a reference dataset (LPDR), the algorithm assumes unbiased SWF, SM and VOD estimations to construct the look-up table. Additionally, for the reference emissivities, the changes over soil and water surface roughness are not accounted.

SWF retrieval approach#

The SWF (Surface Water Fraction) retrieval approach follows the next steps:

  1. Observed missivity calculation: Emissivity is calculated as the K-band TB data from CIMR divided by \(T_{skin}\) temperature from ECMWF.

  2. SWF Calculation through DR: The final SWF is derived using the DR method. This compares the observed emissivity with the reference land emissivity, relative to the emissivity difference between pure water and land. The model is applied exclusively to non-frozen pixels (to be defined in future versions).

Mathematical description#

(3)#\[f_w = \frac{(\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref} - \varepsilon_{h}^{obs})}{(\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref} - \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref})}\]

where \( \varepsilon_{h}^{obs}\) represents the actual satellite emissivity observation for \( h\) polarization, \( \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}\) denotes the emissivity from the reference water endmember pixel and \( \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}\) is the emissivity from the reference land endmember pixel.

Input data#

  • Level-2 Pre-Processed TB (non water-corrected) at 18.7 GHz (h polarization).

  • Sharpened Level-2 CIMR SM.

  • Level-2 VOD at 10.7 GHz.

Output data#

  • CIMR L2 Surface Water Fraction.

  • CIMR L2 Surface Water Fraction uncertainty.

  • CIMR L2 Surface Water Fraction status flag.

Auxiliary data#

  • LUT with land emissivities for different SM and VOD ranges of values.

  • Skin temperature, urban, permanent frozen, permanent water areas and distance to coast.

In the proposed baseline algorithm, ECMWF skin temperature will initially be used as an auxiliary input. However, its use may be reconsidered in the future. If the brightness temperature ratio between the 18.7 GHz and 36 GHz channels yields accurate SWF estimates, the algorithm could operate without relying on this auxiliary input, thereby reducing dependency on external datasets.

A full list of input, output, and auxiliary data is provided in the IODD.

Uncertainty quantification#

The uncertainty characterization for the Surface Water Fraction (SWF) retrieval product is subject to future refinement. The current approach presents an error propagation scheme, based on input uncertainties and partial derivatives.

We assume the following sources of uncertainty:

  • \(TB_{h}\) (brightness temperature): \(\sigma_{TB}\),

  • \(T_{skin}\) (skin temperature from ECMWF): \(\sigma_{T_{skin}}\),

  • \(\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}\) (reference land emissivity) from LUT standard deviations: \(\sigma_{hl}\),

  • \(\varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}\) (reference freshwater emissivity): \(\sigma_{hw}\)

The total uncertainty of the SWF is expressed by its variance:

(4)#\[\sigma^2_{f_w} = \left( \frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_h^{obs}} \right)^2 \sigma^2_{\varepsilon} + \left( \frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}} \right)^2 \sigma^2_{hl} + \left( \frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}} \right)^2 \sigma^2_{hw}\]

where \(\sigma_{\varepsilon}\) is the standard deviation of the observed emissivity, which depends on \(\sigma_{TB}\) and \(\sigma_{T_{skin}}\). The standard deviation \(\sigma_{TB}\) arises from the random noise and calibration uncertainty of the L1B TB. \(\sigma_{hw}\) is static, and \(\sigma_{hl}\) is obtained from a LUT that depends on SM and VOD. The calculation of \(\sigma_{\varepsilon}\) is detailed below.

Partial Derivatives of SWF: Step-by-Step Derivation#

We start from the expression:

(5)#\[f_w = \frac{\varepsilon_h^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}}{\varepsilon_{hw}^{ref} - \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}} = \frac{\varepsilon_h^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}}{\Delta}\]
1. Derivative with respect to \(\varepsilon_h^{obs}\) :#

\(\Delta\) is constant with respect to \(\varepsilon_h^{obs}\):

(6)#\[\frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_h^{obs}} = \frac{1}{\Delta}\]
2. Derivative with respect to \(\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}\) :#
(7)#\[\frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref}} = \frac{-\Delta + (\varepsilon_h^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref})}{\Delta^2} = \frac{\varepsilon_h^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}}{\Delta^2}\]
3. Derivative with respect to \(\varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}\) :#
(8)#\[\frac{\partial f_w}{\partial \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}} = \frac{-(\varepsilon_h^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hl}^{ref})}{\Delta^2} = \frac{\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref} - \varepsilon_{h}^{obs}}{\Delta^2}\]

The total uncertainty of the SWF estimate is then calculated applying error propagation:

(9)#\[\sigma_{f_w}^2 = \left( \frac{1}{\Delta} \right)^2 \sigma_{\varepsilon}^2 + \left( \frac{\varepsilon_{h}^{obs} - \varepsilon_{hw}^{ref}}{\Delta^2} \right)^2 \sigma_{hl}^2 + \left( \frac{\varepsilon_{hl}^{ref} - \varepsilon_{h}^{obs}}{\Delta^2} \right)^2 \sigma_{hw}^2\]

Note that the observed emissivity \(\varepsilon\) is computed from the ratio:

(10)#\[\varepsilon = \frac{TB_{h}}{T_{skin}}\]

To estimate its uncertainty, we apply error propagation:

(11)#\[\sigma_{\varepsilon}^2 = \left( \frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial TB_{h}} \right)^2 \sigma_{TB}^2 + \left( \frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial T_{skin}} \right)^2 \sigma_{T_{skin}}^2\]

The computation of the partial derivatives is:

  • \(\frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial TB_{h}} = \frac{1}{T_{skin}}\)

  • \(\frac{\partial \varepsilon}{\partial T_{skin}} = -\frac{TB_{h}}{T_{skin}^2}\)

Substituting into the formula:

(12)#\[\sigma_{\varepsilon}^2 = \left( \frac{1}{T_{skin}} \right)^2 \sigma_{TB}^2 + \left( \frac{TB_{h}}{T_{skin}^2} \right)^2 \sigma_{T_{skin}}^2\]

Note

Uncertainty values are computed per pixel and propagated into the final SWF_uncertainty output product, alongside a status_flag to indicate suspect input values (e.g. low confidence T_skin).

Output Variables#

  • surface_water_fraction: Main SWF retrieval value.

  • swf_uncertainty: Estimated variance of SWF from analytical error propagation.

  • status_flag: Coded flag for quality control and traceability of uncertainty contributions.
