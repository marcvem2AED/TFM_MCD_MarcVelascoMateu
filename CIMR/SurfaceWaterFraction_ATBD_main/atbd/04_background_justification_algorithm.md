# Background and justification of selected algorithm

In selecting the SWF algorithm in this ATBD, it is critical to place its development within the context of other satellite remote sensing techniques used for monitoring global land surface water dynamics. Historically, satellite remote sensing of SWF has utilized high-resolution optical and infrared (IR) imagery or radar backscatter data for mapping SWF with fine spatial detail but at lower temporal frequencies. These methods, while effective for detailed mapping, often face limitations in terms of temporal coverage and are susceptible to weather-related constraints.

In contrast, passive microwave radiometry, as utilized by sensors like AMSR-E, AMSR2, WindSat and SSM/I, allow higher temporal frequency (daily to 10-day intervals) SWF mapping, though with coarser spatial resolutions. Passive microwaves allow global coverage and the ability to penetrate cloud cover, thus enabling consistent daily global SWF monitoring. However, passive microwave sensing faces challenges in accurately detecting surface water under dense vegetation canopies, a limitation that becomes more pronounced at higher microwave frequencies {cite:p}`Prigent2007,Du2016`.

The approach that is presented here is based on the contribution by {cite:p}`Du2018`, that developed the L-band SWF algorithm, which builds upon the foundational principles of passive microwave radiometry to estimate the fractional cover of land surface water, called SWF in this document. By focusing on the L-band (1.4 GHz) frequency, known for its enhanced canopy penetration capabilities, the L-band SWF algorithm aimed to correct for the underestimation of surface water extent in vegetated areas, a known constraint of previous methodologies that utilized higher microwave frequencies. The development of the L-band SWF algorithm originated from a methodology initially applied to AMSR-E W-band (89 GHz) Brightness Temperature (TB) measurements for identifying pan-Arctic flooding patterns {cite:p}`Du2016`. In this approach, a first step involved creating a look-up table (LUT) to characterize 89 GHz microwave emissivities for pure land and water endmembers under a range of global land and atmosphere conditions. The W-band SWF data were then derived for individual pixels by calculating the composite H-pol and V-pol double difference ratio (DDR) TB that accounts for emissivity variations from the baseline scenarios designated for land and water surface types {cite:p}`Du2016`. The W-band SWF retrieval is complementary to other approaches as the K-band SWF retrieval, that uses a model that exploits the capabilities of the 18.7 and 23.8 GHz bands from AMSR-E {cite:p}`Jones2010`. The latter is the basis of the SWF retrieval available in the land parameter data record (LPDR, {cite:p}`Du2017`). The LPDR provide a long-term (June 2002–December 2015) global record of key environmental observations at a 25 km grid cell resolution, including SWF, atmosphere precipitable water vapor (PWV), daily maximum and minimum surface air temperatures (Tmx and Tmn), vegetation optical depth (VOD) and soil moisture (SM). 

The baseline SWF algorithm for CIMR are based on an approach similar to L-band SWF proposed by {cite:p}`Du2018`, and K-band SWF, that applied the DDR method proposed by {cite:p}`Du2016`. In the SWF ATBD, the algorithm will be applied to CIMR K-band (18.7 GHz) TB data since it provides a high spatial resolution and a lower impact of strong atmospheric effects (e.g. the absorption caused by oxygen, clouds and precipitable rain) than Ka-band. This selection is supported by previous existing methods already tested at this specific band for SWF estimation {cite:p}`Du2017`. Furthermore, the use of a single H-polarization Difference Ratio (DR) is proposed instead of a Double Difference Ratio (DDR), since preliminary tests have shown that models based solely on horizontal polarization achieve higher accuracy. In addition, tests using only the Ka-band (36.5 GHz, H and V pol.) resulted in lower accuracy, as did a model using the ratio between the K and Ka bands. This approach is further supported by {cite:p}`Du2018`, who reported that the DDR method exhibits greater retrieval uncertainties than the DR approach in sparsely vegetated and barren regions, where relatively large V and H polarization differences resemble the characteristics of open water emissions.
